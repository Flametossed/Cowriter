The fog came up Candle Street the way it always did, slow, patient, feeling for the windows. I watched it from the sill. Below me the lamps were guttering one by one, and in each dying flame a face turned to look at me before it went.

I did not wave. You learn not to wave.

Mother's lamp still burned on the table where I had left it. Brass, dented at the base, the wick trimmed the way she liked. I had not let it go out in three years. Some nights I told myself that was love. Other nights I knew it for what it was, which was fear wearing love's coat, and on those nights I trimmed the wick anyway.

Downstairs my father's cane found the boards. Two taps, a drag. He was awake. He was always awake now. The plague had taken his sleep before it took anything else, and the city did not yet know what it had lost.
