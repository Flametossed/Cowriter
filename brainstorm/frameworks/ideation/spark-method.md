# Spark Method — divergence

> Portable. Generate **many** raw story concepts from a writer's seed (or from nothing). This is the *divergent* half of brainstorming: make a pile, judge nothing. Output goes to the session log's **Sparks** list.

> The single rule: **defer judgment.** Quantity, specificity, and weirdness now; criticism later in `twist-method` and `originality-tests`. If you catch yourself (or the writer) saying "that's been done," note it and keep going — that's a convergence move arriving early.

---

## Why AI ideas feel generic (the thing we're fighting)

Asked for a story, a model returns the *center of mass* of everything it has read: the most probable concept given the prompt. That center is, by definition, the most familiar thing — the chosen one, the ancient evil, the reluctant hero. Originality is **deliberately moving off the center while staying coherent.** The fastest way off-center is a specific the average story would never contain — and the cheapest source of those is the *writer*, not the genre.

---

## The five generators

Use **one at a time.** Offer the writer a generator to react to rather than asking them to invent cold. Run a generator until it yields 2–4 sparks, then switch. Target **8–12 sparks** before anyone picks.

### 1. Mine the writer (start here, especially from a blank)
The richest, least-generic material is already in the writer's head. Ask for one at a time:
- An obsession or rabbit hole they lose hours to.
- A real memory with an unresolved charge — something that still itches.
- A belief they hold that most people would argue with.
- An image, place, or object that won't leave them.
- A job, subculture, or skill they know from the inside that fiction rarely shows accurately.
Turn each answer into a spark by asking: *"What story can only happen because of this detail?"* A protagonist who restores pipe organs; a town that votes on the weather; a grief that arrives as a tax audit. The specific carries the originality.

### 2. The What-If ladder
Take any what-if and climb. The first answer is always the trope — keep going:
- *What if the dead could vote?* → (rung 1, default) ghosts haunt elections. *And the version I haven't seen?* → (rung 2) the dead form a voting bloc large enough that the living are a minority. *And past that?* → (rung 3) a politician's whole platform is dying constituents he intends to keep represented forever.
Climb at least **three rungs.** The trope lives on rung 1; the story lives on rung 3.

### 3. Forced collision
Take two unrelated domains, words, or images and find the premise at the intersection. Pull them far apart — closeness yields cliché, distance yields novelty:
- *competitive cake decorating* × *grief counseling*
- *deep-sea cartography* × *inheritance law*
- *a monastery* × *speedrunning*
The collision forces a world that doesn't pre-exist in the trope library.

### 4. Constraint injection
Add one arbitrary, hard, world-level rule and ask what story it forces:
- *No one in this world can lie.* *Money decays like fruit.* *Gravity reverses one day a week.* *The dead age but the living don't.*
A hard constraint generates plot for free, because every scene has to obey it. The weirder the rule, the further off-center the story.

### 5. Invert the given
Take the obvious version of the seed and flip an axis:
- **Power:** make the powerful party the underdog (the empire is dying; the rebels won and now must govern).
- **Victim/agent:** tell it from the monster, the bystander, the prophesied one's mother.
- **Goal:** the hero wants the thing the trope says is evil.
- **Outcome:** the prophecy is fulfilled in act one — now what?

## How to run a session

1. Pick a generator that fits the seed (blank → mine-the-writer; a vibe → what-if ladder or collision; a half-idea → invert-the-given on that idea).
2. Produce 2–4 sparks, **one line each**, unpolished. A spark is a *seed of conflict*, not a finished premise — "a town that votes on the weather and the vote is rigged" is enough.
3. Capture every spark in the log. Don't lose the bad ones; a "bad" spark often donates a detail to a good one.
4. Switch generators when a vein runs dry. Cross-pollinate: take a detail from spark #3 into the constraint from spark #7.
5. Stop at 8–12. Read the list back. Ask the convergence question that ends divergence: **"Which 1–2 have heat — not which is best, which won't let you go?"**

---

## Anti-patterns (signs divergence has gone wrong)

- **Polishing too early** — writing paragraphs for spark #1 instead of generating spark #2. Pile first.
- **Judging mid-pile** — "that's derivative" belongs in Twist. Note it, move on.
- **Genre tunnel** — every spark is the same subgenre. Force a generator that breaks frame (collision with a far domain).
- **Abstraction** — "a story about loss." Not a spark. Push for the concrete situation that *causes* the loss.
- **Stopping at three** — the good ideas usually live past #6, after the obvious ones are spent.
