# Plot Method — building an original engine

> Portable. Turn a locked premise into an original **plot engine** — the self-sustaining source of escalation, not a borrowed structure. Reads the premise from the session log; writes the chosen engine + first move back. Hands the engine to `beat-method`, then to Cowriter's outline.

> The distinction that matters: a **structure** (3-act, Save the Cat) is a *container*; an **engine** is what *generates pressure* inside it. AI plots feel generic because they borrow a structure and fill it with default turns. Build the engine first; the structure comes later (in Cowriter).

---

## What an engine is

An engine is the mechanism that makes the next scene *inevitable and worse*. It comes from **this story's specific rules**, not from a beat template. Three parts:

1. **The irreversible first move** — the thing that happens early that *can't be undone*, that forecloses the safe path. Not "she sets out on a journey" (reversible) but "she spends the inheritance that was supposed to bury her father" (closed door).
2. **The escalation logic** — *why* each situation forces a worse one, drawn from the premise's own constraints/costs. If the magic costs memory, the engine is: every use makes her less able to recognize what she's fighting for.
3. **The squeeze** — the two pressures that close on the protagonist from opposite sides, so every choice pays one by betraying the other.

A premise with no engine describes a *situation*; you can summarize it but you can't see what happens next. That's the failure to catch here.

---

## Method

1. **Read the premise** (logline, three-layer conflict, the central cost). The engine must serve all three layers — external plot, internal arc, philosophical stake.

2. **Find the irreversible first move.** Ask: *what does the protagonist do or lose, early, that closes the exit?* Generate three candidates; pick the one that most forecloses retreat.

3. **Generate 3 candidate engines**, each with a *different* escalation logic:
   - one driven by the **world's cost/rule** (the system extracts a price that compounds),
   - one driven by a **relationship/loyalty squeeze** (helping one person betrays another),
   - one driven by the **protagonist's own flaw** (the Lie they believe keeps making it worse).
   State each as: *"Every time X, it forces Y, until Z becomes unavoidable."*

4. **Pick by the flaw test.** Choose the engine that best forces the protagonist's internal flaw into the open — the one where the *plot* and the *arc* are the same pressure. (This is the seam Cowriter's character method picks up: Want / Need / Lie / Ghost. The best engine makes the external goal and the internal Lie collide.)

5. **Trope-check the spine** with `trope-slop`. Specifically hunt the default plot turns:
   - Is the midpoint a mentor's death? a betrayal-reveal? Replace if reflexive.
   - Is the climax a self-sacrifice that gets undone? a power-of-love override? a final-boss duel? Find the turn this *specific* engine actually demands instead.
   - Is the all-is-lost beat there because the structure expects one, or because the engine produced it? Only keep engine-produced beats.

6. **Pressure-test escalation.** Walk the engine forward five steps. If you reach a flat stretch ("and then more of the same happens"), the engine is too weak — the squeeze isn't tightening. Add a second pressure or a compounding cost.

---

## Output (to the log)

- **Engine (one line):** *"Every time she ___, it forces ___, until ___ is unavoidable."*
- **Irreversible first move:** ...
- **The squeeze:** pressure A vs. pressure B.
- **Why this engine fits the arc:** how plot pressure = the Lie under stress.

Keep it a hypothesis, not a contract — Cowriter's outline turns it into beats and the structure choice happens there.
