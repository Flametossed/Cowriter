# Twist Method — the originality engine

> Portable. Take one chosen spark — usually still sitting on a familiar default — and make it **un-AI**. This is the *convergent* turn: judgment switches on. Reads from the session log's Sparks; writes the twisted concept back.

> The discipline: **name the default before you subvert it.** You can't move off-center until you've said out loud where the center is. Then twist **one** axis hard, and protect the spark's heat — never twist away the thing that excited the writer.

---

## Step 1 — Name the default (do this first, always)

Write the version a model or the market would produce by reflex from this spark. Be honest and specific:

> Spark: *"a young mage discovers a hidden power."*
> Default: chosen-one farm kid, secret royal blood, ancient evil stirs, wise mentor dies at the midpoint, power-of-love climax.

The default is now a **target, not a plan.** Everything below moves away from it. Run `trope-slop` (trope-check) here to surface defaults the spark is leaning on that you didn't notice.

---

## Step 2 — Pick ONE subversion lever

Originality is **one axis of weird on top of a familiar promise.** Twisting every axis at once produces an unsellable mess; twisting none leaves slop. Choose **one** lever and commit:

### Invert the trope
Flip the trope's load-bearing assumption. The chosen one *refuses*, or is a decoy, or was chosen by mistake and the real one is the antagonist. The prophecy is fulfilled on page 10. The mentor is the threat.

### Move the camera
Keep the trope world; change whose story it is. The epic from the quartermaster's ledger. The haunted house from the ghost's side. The chosen-one saga told by the kid who *wasn't* chosen and has to live next door to destiny.

### Change the engine / cost
Make the *price* of the premise's central system the actual subject. If there's magic, what it costs is the story (memory? years? someone else's?). The mechanism stops being color and becomes the conflict.

### Relocate (time or domain)
Same human situation, unexpected setting. The locked-room mystery on a generation ship's compost deck. The coming-of-age inside a dying MMO's economy. The war story set thirty years *after*, among the people cleaning it up.

### Literalize / metaphorize
Make a metaphor physically real ("carrying grief" → grief has mass and you're weighed at the door), or render a literal thing as the story's metaphor (a real estate dispute that is actually about who gets to define home).

---

## Step 3 — Protect the heat

Before regenerating, name **the thing that made the writer pick this spark** — the image, the feeling, the question. The twist must keep that. If a lever guts the heat, it's the wrong lever; pick another. Originality that bores its own author is worthless.

---

## Step 4 — The specificity pass

Walk the twisted concept and replace every generic noun with a load-bearing specific one:
- "a kingdom" → a named place with **one rule that defines it** (a city where citizenship is inherited debt).
- "a dark force" → a particular, ownable threat with a logic (a bureaucracy that forecloses on souls in arrears).
- "a hero" → this person, with the one trait that makes them the *only* one this plot could happen to.

Each generic noun you leave in is a missed choice and a slide back toward the center.

---

## Step 5 — Show, steer, lock

Show the twisted concept in 2–4 sentences. If the writer doesn't like it, run the conductor's **Steer loop**: pin whether it's *too familiar* (pick a stronger lever) or *too weird* (re-anchor in a familiar promise, keep one twist axis). Log: the default named, the lever used, kept vs. killed, and the twisted concept.

---

## Calibration: the familiarity dial

Originality is a **dial, not a maximum.** Readers buy the familiar wrapped around one surprise.

```
all familiar ───────────────●───────────── all novel
   = slop          (aim here: 1 strong twist)      = unsellable
```

- **Anchor:** one promise the reader recognizes and wants (a heist, a haunting, a first love).
- **Twist:** one axis bent hard enough to be unmistakably new.
- **Everything else:** can stay conventional — that's the wrapper that makes the twist legible.

If two readers couldn't agree on what the *anchor* is, you've twisted too much. If neither can name the *twist*, you haven't twisted at all.
