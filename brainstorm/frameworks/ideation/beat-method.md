# Beat Method — subverting the turns

> Portable. Sketch the major beats of the story so each delivers its structural *job* without the cliché *execution*. Reads the premise + plot engine from the session log; writes a beat sketch back, shaped for handoff to Cowriter's `outline-method`.

> This is **not** outlining — Cowriter does the full beat sheet, structure choice, and per-beat cross-checks. Brainstormer's job is narrower and upstream: make sure the *turns* aren't the predictable ones before the outline locks them in.

---

## The principle: same job, different move

Every beat exists to do a **structural job** — introduce the world, force a commitment, reverse a fortune, strip the protagonist down, pay off a setup. The job is non-negotiable; the *move* that performs it is wide open. AI beats feel generic because they reach for the single most common move for each job:

| Job | Default move (the cliché) | The opening |
|-----|---------------------------|-------------|
| Inciting incident | A stranger/letter summons the hero to destiny | What's the version they *cause*, not receive? |
| Refusal → commitment | "I'm not the hero" then forced anyway | What if they commit eagerly, for the *wrong* reason? |
| Midpoint reversal | A betrayal reveal, or the mentor dies | What turn does *this engine* actually produce? |
| All is lost | Everything taken; hero at rock bottom | What if they *get what they wanted* and it's the disaster? |
| Climax | Final duel; self-sacrifice; love conquers | What cost does the premise's own logic demand? |
| Resolution | Order restored; the world healed | What's permanently changed and can't go back? |

---

## Method

1. **List the major turns the engine produces** — not a structure's beat names, but the points where the squeeze tightens. Usually 5–8 for a novel sketch. (Cowriter will map these onto 3-act / Save the Cat / etc. later.)

2. **For each turn, do the three-move drill:**
   - **Name the default move** — the most likely execution for this job. Say it out loud.
   - **Generate 2 alternatives** that do the *same job* by a *different move*, drawn from this story's specifics (its world rule, its protagonist's flaw, its squeeze).
   - **Pick the one with heat** that the engine actually justifies — not the weirdest, the *truest* to this machine.

3. **Run the predictability check.** Read the beats in order. For each beat ask: *could a reader predict this from the previous beat?* If yes, the story has gone slack — bend the beat so it satisfies the setup in an *unexpected but inevitable* way ("surprising yet earned" is the target; surprising-but-arbitrary is just as dead as predictable).

4. **Protect the payoffs.** Subversion that breaks a setup is a cheat, not a twist. Every surprising beat must still *pay off* something planted earlier — it changes the *how*, never welshes on the promise.

5. **Trope-check the sequence** with `trope-slop` — especially the resurrection-undo, the villain monologue, the "it was all a dream/simulation," and the romantic subplot that resolves on cue.

---

## Output (to the log)

A beat sketch, one block per turn:

```
### Turn <n> — <the structural job>
- Default move: <the cliché we're avoiding>
- Chosen move: <what happens instead — 1–2 sentences>
- Pays off: <what earlier setup this satisfies>
- Why it's not predictable: <one line>
```

This is a **sketch, not an outline.** It hands to Cowriter's `outline-method`, which chooses the structure, expands each turn into full beats with value-shifts and POV, and cross-checks against the bible. Keep it loose — you're guaranteeing the turns are fresh, not finalizing them.
